Approach

Technology should host, not dominate

"If this became my third place, how would I want it to feel?"

That's the question I ask on every project. Third spaces—museums, visitor centers, brand homes—are where people show up as themselves and slowly build a sense of "this is our place." When I design an installation, I'm asking: does this make it easier for people to linger, talk, and belong?

Public technology cuts both ways. Done well, large interactives become intuitive interfaces where visitors steer their own journey. Done poorly, they're confusing gates that shut people out—especially elders, kids, and anyone who doesn't live inside screens all day.

I design installations so tech behaves like a good host: clear affordances, gentle error states, multiple ways to engage, and fallback stories that still work offline. Behind the scenes, I build content structures and systems so staff can update and run things without a PhD in the stack.
